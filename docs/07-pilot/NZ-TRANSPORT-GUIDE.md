# New Zealand Transport Guide

## Overview

Transport planning is central to the NZ travel experience. Most international visitors self-drive (rental car or campervan), and the journey between destinations is often as memorable as the destinations themselves. NZ's compact size means driving times are manageable, but winding roads, single-lane bridges, and variable conditions mean journey times are longer than distance alone suggests.

Buddi needs deep transport knowledge to: generate realistic itineraries, manage ferry bookings, recommend appropriate vehicle types, warn about road conditions, and adjust plans when roads close.

### Self-Contained Certification

A critical concept for NZ campervan travel. A "self-contained" vehicle has a fixed toilet, grey water tank, and fresh water supply that allows multi-day independence from external facilities.

**Why it matters:**
- Only self-contained vehicles are legally allowed to freedom camp in most areas
- Non-self-contained vehicles must stay in designated camping grounds with toilet facilities
- The NZ government has increasingly cracked down on non-compliant freedom camping
- Blue "self-contained" warrant sticker must be displayed on the vehicle

**Buddi's role:** Know the vehicle's self-contained status and filter camping recommendations accordingly. A non-self-contained Jucy Crib has different overnight options than a self-contained Maui motorhome.

### Freedom Camping

Free camping on public land — a significant part of NZ campervan culture, but increasingly regulated.

**Rules (as of 2024):**
- Must be in a self-contained vehicle (national law, Freedom Camping Act 2011, amended 2023)
- Many councils have designated freedom camping areas — use the CamperMate or Rankers app
- Some areas prohibit freedom camping entirely (Queenstown, much of Auckland)
- Leave no trace: no litter, no dumping, no damage
- Maximum stay typically 2 nights in one spot
- Fines of $200+ for non-compliance, enforced via rangers and cameras

**Best freedom camping areas (South Island):**
- Lake Tekapo foreshore (designated area, stunning views)
- Various West Coast pulloffs (the West Coast is generally more freedom-camping friendly)
- Selected NZTA rest areas along SH6 and SH73

**Best freedom camping areas (North Island):**
- More restricted overall, especially around Auckland, Coromandel, and Rotorua
- Some Bay of Plenty and East Cape coastal spots
- Taupō has some designated lakeside areas

### Campervan Tips for Buddi

- Always suggest booking peak-season campervan 3-6 months ahead
- Recommend 2-berth for couples (4-berth is tempting but harder to park and drive)
- One-way rentals (e.g., Auckland pick-up, Christchurch drop-off) incur $100-250 relocation fees
- Vehicles may need chains for mountain passes in winter (Crown Range, Arthur's Pass)
- Large motorhomes (6-berth) cannot access some roads (e.g., Skippers Canyon)
- Dump stations for waste tanks are at most holiday parks and some service stations

### Insurance

**Critical consideration.** NZ has a no-fault accident system (ACC) for personal injury, but vehicle damage is the renter's responsibility.

| Coverage | What It Covers | Daily Cost |
|----------|---------------|-----------|
| **Standard (included)** | Third-party liability, glass/tires often excluded | Included |
| **Excess Reduction** | Reduces excess from $2,000-3,000 to $200-500 | $25-40 NZD/day |
| **Full Cover / Zero Excess** | Zero excess, includes glass/tires/undercarriage | $35-55 NZD/day |
| **Third-party (your own travel insurance)** | Check your policy — many cover rental car excess | Varies |

**Buddi recommendation:** Always suggest checking travel insurance coverage first. Many comprehensive travel insurance policies include rental vehicle excess coverage, saving $25-55/day on unnecessary add-ons.

### Driving in New Zealand

**Driving side:** Left-hand side (right-hand drive vehicles). This is the single biggest adjustment for visitors from continental Europe, the Americas, and most of Asia.

**Key driving rules:**
- Speed limits: 100 km/h open road, 50 km/h urban
- Blood alcohol limit: 50 mg/100 ml (lower than many countries)
- Seatbelts mandatory for all occupants
- Headlights required at all times (newer vehicles have auto daytime running lights)
- Give way to traffic crossing or turning right at uncontrolled intersections (opposite to right-hand driving countries)
- Single-lane bridges are common — yield signs indicate who has right of way
- Roundabouts: give way to traffic already on the roundabout (from the right)

**Common driving mistakes by international visitors:**
1. Drifting to the wrong side, especially after stops or on quiet rural roads
2. Underestimating driving times on winding roads
3. Not using passing bays on single-lane roads (pull over to let faster traffic pass)
4. Driving too fast on gravel roads
5. Fatigue — long scenic drives are tiring due to constant steering input

### Road Conditions

| Road Type | Where | Notes |
|-----------|-------|-------|
| **State Highways (SH)** | Main routes | Generally well-maintained, two-lane (not motorway outside cities). SH1 is the "main" route. |
| **Sealed secondary roads** | Between towns | Good quality but narrower. Single-lane bridges common. |
| **Gravel roads** | Remote areas, back roads | Common to many attractions. Drive at 40-60 km/h. Rental insurance may not cover gravel road damage. |
| **Mountain passes** | Alpine crossings | Arthur's Pass, Crown Range, Milford Road, Lindis Pass. Can close in winter. Chains may be required. |

**Road closures:** Check Waka Kotahi / NZTA Journey Planner (journeys.nzta.govt.nz) for real-time road conditions.

### One-Way Fees

Most rental companies charge for one-way rentals (e.g., pick up Auckland, drop off Queenstown):

| Route | Typical One-Way Fee |
|-------|-------------------|

| Same island, different city (e.g., Auckland → Wellington) | $50-100 NZD |

**Buddi tip:** Factor one-way fees into total cost comparisons. Sometimes a round-trip rental + domestic flight is cheaper than a one-way rental.

The Cook Strait crossing between the North and South Islands is a key part of most NZ trips. Two operators run between Wellington and Picton.

### Costs (Approximate)

| Passenger/Vehicle | Interislander | Bluebridge |
|-------------------|--------------|-----------|
| Adult foot passenger | $65-85 NZD | $55-75 NZD |
| Car (up to 5.5 m) | $135-250 NZD | $120-200 NZD |
| Campervan (5.5-7 m) | $180-350 NZD | $160-300 NZD |
| Large motorhome (7-8 m) | $220-400 NZD | $200-380 NZD |

Prices vary by sailing time (peak vs off-peak departures), day of week, and advance booking.

### Booking Tips

- **Book 2-4 weeks ahead in peak season** — popular sailings (morning departures, school holidays) sell out
- **Flexible timing saves money** — afternoon and late-night sailings are cheapest
- **Vehicle dimensions matter** — measure your campervan before booking; oversize fees apply
- **Bad weather cancellations** — Cook Strait is notoriously rough. Sailings cancel in severe weather (typically 5-10 days per year). Buddi should monitor conditions and warn travelers.
- **Allow buffer time** — don't plan tight connections on either side of the ferry. Arrive 60 min before departure.
- **The crossing itself is scenic** — Marlborough Sounds on the Picton end are stunning in calm weather

### Weather Disruptions

Cook Strait sits between two landmasses in the Roaring Forties. Strong southerlies and northwesterlies can create 4-6 m swells, leading to:
- Sailing cancellations (typically 1-2 day disruptions)
- Severe delays
- Very uncomfortable crossings (motion sickness is common)

**Buddi's role:** Monitor marine forecasts for Cook Strait. If conditions look rough, suggest:
1. Taking an earlier/later sailing in calmer conditions
2. Adjusting the itinerary to allow a buffer day
3. Considering a domestic flight as an alternative (vehicles can be returned and re-rented)
4. Reminding travelers to bring seasickness medication

**Regional routes:** Auckland ↔ Rotorua, Taupō, Napier, Hamilton, Tauranga; Christchurch ↔ Dunedin, Invercargill, Hokitika; Wellington ↔ Nelson, Blenheim, New Plymouth

### Jetstar

Budget carrier operating on the busiest domestic routes (Auckland ↔ Christchurch, Wellington, Queenstown, Dunedin). Often cheaper than Air NZ but with carry-on baggage restrictions.

### Sounds Air

Small regional airline flying between Wellington ↔ Picton/Blenheim/Nelson. An alternative to the ferry crossing (30 min flight vs 3.5 hr ferry). No vehicles, foot passengers only with limited luggage.

### Stewart Island Flights

Stewart Island Flights operates between Invercargill and Ryan's Creek Airstrip on Stewart Island. 20-minute scenic flight in a small Cessna. Books out in peak season.

### Baggage for Adventure Gear

### EV Charging Infrastructure

NZ's EV charging network is growing but not yet comprehensive for touring:

- **ChargeNet** — Largest network, DC fast chargers on main highways
- **Tesla Superchargers** — Select locations on main routes (Tesla vehicles only)
- **Destination chargers** — Many hotels and holiday parks have AC chargers

**Current limitations for touring:**
- West Coast has very limited charging infrastructure
- Remote areas (Haast, Catlins, East Cape) may not have any chargers
- Charging an EV campervan is impractical (very high energy consumption, few high-powered chargers)
- Buddi should not recommend EV for remote touring routes without verifying charger availability

### Key Rule of Thumb

**Multiply Google Maps estimated times by 1.2-1.5** for NZ driving. Reasons:
- Winding roads mean average speeds of 60-80 km/h, not the posted 100 km/h
- Single-lane bridges create delays
- Scenic stops are irresistible (and should be encouraged)
- Campervans travel slower than cars (especially large motorhomes)
- Tourist traffic slows popular routes in peak season

**Buddi should build in 20-30% time buffer** on all driving segments and suggest rest/photo stops along the way.
