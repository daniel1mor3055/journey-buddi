# New Zealand Weather Patterns — Detailed Guide for Trip Planning

## General Climate

New Zealand has a **maritime temperate climate** — surrounded by ocean, strongly influenced by the prevailing westerly wind belt, and characterized by moderate temperatures but high variability. The country sits between latitudes 34°S (Northland) and 47°S (Stewart Island), straddling the Roaring Forties — one of the most active weather zones on Earth.

### Key Climate Characteristics

- **Maritime influence:** No point is more than 130 km from the sea. This moderates temperature extremes but brings frequent weather changes.
- **Changeable:** "Four seasons in one day" is not hyperbole. Frontal systems cross the country every 3-7 days, bringing rapid transitions between clear, cloudy, wet, and windy conditions.
- **Rain shadow:** The Southern Alps create a dramatic west-east rainfall gradient. The West Coast receives 3-6x more rain than eastern areas at the same latitude.
- **UV intensity:** NZ has some of the highest UV radiation levels globally due to its position relative to the Antarctic ozone hole, clear air, and high altitude sun angles in summer. The UV Index regularly reaches 12-13 in summer (Extreme).
- **Wind:** The prevailing westerlies are a constant factor. Wellington is one of the windiest cities on Earth. Wind affects activity feasibility more than temperature in many cases.

**Buddi note:** West Coast rain should not be presented as universally negative. Many experiences (rainforest walks, Ship Creek, Hokitika Gorge) are enhanced by rain. Glaciers need clear sky for heli-hikes but the valley walks are fine in drizzle.

## Wind Patterns

### Prevailing Westerlies

NZ sits in the prevailing westerly wind belt. The dominant weather pattern is:
1. Weather systems approach from the west (Tasman Sea)
2. Hit the Southern Alps and dump moisture (West Coast rain)
3. Descend as warm, dry foehn winds on the eastern side (Canterbury nor'westers)
4. Systems continue east, new one approaches from the west in 3-7 days

### Roaring Forties Impact

The latitude band 40-50°S is known for strong, persistent westerly winds with few land masses to slow them. The South Island sits squarely in this band, making wind a constant planning factor.

**Wind speed categories for Buddi:**

| Category | Speed | Impact |
|----------|-------|--------|
| Calm | 0-15 km/h | Ideal for all activities, mirror-lake conditions possible |
| Moderate | 15-30 km/h | Fine for most activities, kayaking manageable, slight chop on water |
| Breezy | 30-45 km/h | Exposed hiking uncomfortable, kayaking challenging, some boat trips affected |
| Windy | 45-60 km/h | Alpine hiking risky, water activities cancelled, campervans affected |
| Gale | 60+ km/h | Most outdoor activities unsafe, driving large vehicles dangerous, ferries may cancel |

### Wind Impact on Specific Activities

| Activity | Wind Threshold | Notes |
|----------|---------------|-------|
| Kayaking (sheltered bay) | Cancel at 25+ km/h | Abel Tasman, Okarito — sheltered but still affected |
| Kayaking (open water) | Cancel at 20+ km/h | Lake Taupō, open coast |
| Glacier heli-hike | Cancel at 30+ km/h (gusts) | Helicopters very wind-sensitive |
| Skydiving | Cancel at 30+ km/h | Need stable air for safe landings |
| Tongariro Crossing | Dangerous at 50+ km/h | Exposed ridges, volcanic terrain, hypothermia risk |
| Whale watching cruise | Cancel at 40+ km/h | Rough seas prevent sailing |
| Paragliding | Cancel at 35+ km/h | Need smooth, predictable air |
| Mt Cook viewing | Low wind preferred | But Mt Cook creates its own weather — cloud cap even on calm days |
| Milford Sound cruise | Rarely cancelled | Sheltered fiord, only extreme conditions affect service |
| Freedom camping | Uncomfortable at 40+ km/h | Campervans rock, awnings tear, cooking outside is impossible |

## UV Levels

NZ receives significantly higher UV radiation than equivalent Northern Hemisphere latitudes due to:
1. **Ozone layer thinning** — the Antarctic ozone hole extends its influence over NZ, especially in spring/summer
2. **Clean air** — less atmospheric pollution to filter UV
3. **Sun-Earth distance** — Earth is closest to the sun in January (NZ summer)

### UV Index by Season

| Season | Typical Peak UV Index | Risk Level | Sun Protection |
|--------|----------------------|-----------|----------------|
| Summer (Dec-Feb) | 11-13 | Extreme | Burn time for fair skin: 10-15 min. SPF50+, hat, sunglasses, cover-up essential |
| Autumn (Mar-May) | 4-8 | Moderate-High | Still need protection, especially at altitude |
| Winter (Jun-Aug) | 1-3 | Low-Moderate | Minimal risk except at ski fields (snow reflection) |
| Spring (Sep-Nov) | 5-10 | High-Very High | UV rises rapidly in October; spring UV catches travelers off-guard |

**Buddi tip:** Always include UV warnings in morning briefings, especially for fair-skinned travelers. "You can burn in 10 minutes today — reapply sunscreen every 2 hours."

## Snow and Ice

### Where and When

| Location | Snow Season | Accumulation | Impact |
|----------|-----------|--------------|--------|
| **Southern Alps (>1,500 m)** | Year-round above ~2,500 m; seasonal above 1,000 m | Heavy | Glacier feeding, alpine track closures, avalanche risk |
| **Queenstown ski fields** | Jun-Oct | Good — Coronet Peak, Remarkables | Ski season, road chains sometimes needed |
| **Wanaka ski fields** | Jun-Oct | Good — Cardrona, Treble Cone | Similar to Queenstown |
| **Mt Ruapehu** | Jun-Oct | Variable — Whakapapa, Tūroa | Only North Island ski option |
| **Canterbury high country** | Jun-Sep | Moderate to heavy | Road closures: Arthur's Pass, Lewis Pass, Lindis Pass |
| **Southland/Fiordland** | Jun-Sep | Heavy at altitude | Milford Road closures, Kepler Track closed |
| **Central Plateau (NI)** | Jun-Aug | Light to moderate | Desert Road (SH1) can close, Tongariro area icy |

### Road Chain Requirements

Chains may be legally required on the following routes during winter:
- Crown Range Road (Queenstown ↔ Wanaka)
- Arthur's Pass (SH73)
- Lewis Pass (SH7)
- Lindis Pass (SH8)
- Milford Road (SH94)
- Desert Road (SH1, Tongariro)
- Porters Pass (SH73, Canterbury)

**Buddi should:** Monitor Waka Kotahi road alerts for chain requirements and closures. Suggest chain hire or alternative routes. Large campervans should avoid Crown Range in winter.

## How Weather Fronts Move Through NZ

### Typical Front Passage

```
Day 0: Front approaches from Tasman Sea
  → West Coast: rain starts, wind picks up
  → East Coast: still clear, possibly warm foehn conditions

Day 0-1: Front crosses the Alps
  → West Coast: heavy rain, possible flooding
  → Alps: snow above 1,000-1,500 m (winter)
  → East Coast: cloud increasing, wind change approaching

Day 1: Front reaches east coast
  → West Coast: clearing behind the front
  → East Coast: rain, southerly wind change
  → Temperature drop 5-10°C

Day 1-2: Post-frontal clearance
  → West Coast: clearing, sunshine returns
  → East Coast: showers easing, clearing
  → Brief period of clear weather before next front
```

### Buddi's Weather Window Strategy

The gap between fronts (typically 2-5 days) creates "weather windows" — periods of clear, settled conditions ideal for weather-sensitive activities. Buddi should:

1. **Monitor front timing:** Know when the next front arrives and plan around it
2. **West-to-east time lag:** If the West Coast is clearing, the East Coast will clear 6-12 hours later
3. **Post-front windows:** The day after a front passes is often the clearest — cold, crisp air with excellent visibility
4. **Pre-front conditions:** The day before a front arrives can be warm and clear (especially east of the Alps), creating a window for alpine activities
5. **Multi-front stacking:** Sometimes fronts arrive in quick succession (every 1-2 days) — these periods require pivot planning to indoor/all-weather activities

| Product | Update Frequency | Use Case |
|---------|-----------------|----------|
| Town forecasts | 2x daily (6 AM, 6 PM) | Daily planning, briefings |
| Hourly forecasts | Hourly | Intra-day activity timing |

| Rural forecasts | Daily | General regional conditions |

**API Access:** MetService offers a commercial API with JSON endpoints. Pricing is per-request. For MVP, consider combining free MetService web data with Open-Meteo API for structured forecasts.

**Advantages:** Free, no API key needed, excellent JSON API, global coverage. Good enough for MVP condition scoring with MetService as the authoritative supplement for warnings and mountain forecasts.

### Weather Station Density

NZ has reasonable weather station coverage for a country its size, but gaps exist:

**Good coverage:** Major towns, airports, ski fields, some DOC huts
**Sparse coverage:** Remote West Coast, Fiordland interior, parts of East Cape, Stewart Island

This means forecast accuracy varies by location. Buddi should have higher confidence in forecasts for Queenstown (well-monitored) than for Haast (sparse monitoring).

## Weather Impact on Popular Activities

### Milford Sound — Rain Is Good

**Common traveler mistake:** Cancelling or dreading a Milford Sound trip because of rain.

**Reality:** Milford Sound receives ~6,800 mm of rain per year (182 rain days). If you only visit on clear days, you'll probably never visit. More importantly, rain creates the experience — hundreds of temporary waterfalls cascade down sheer cliff faces, mist swirls around Mitre Peak, and the fiord takes on an otherworldly, primeval atmosphere.

**Clear day advantage:** Mountain reflections in mirror-still water, epic photo conditions, warmer.

**Buddi approach:** Present both options positively. "Today Milford Sound is rainy — which means spectacular waterfalls and atmospheric mist. This is actually how many photographers prefer it."

### Tongariro Alpine Crossing — Wind Is Dangerous

**The safety issue:** The Tongariro Alpine Crossing traverses exposed volcanic terrain above the bushline. Wind chill, disorientation in cloud, ice on the track, and steep volcanic scree create genuine hazards.

**Red flags (recommend cancellation):**
- Wind speed >50 km/h at altitude (MetService mountain forecast)
- Visibility <100 m
- Temperature + wind chill below -5°C
- Snow or ice on track (check DOC alerts)
- Active volcanic alert level >2

**Buddi approach:** Hard safety gate. Do not suggest the crossing in dangerous conditions, regardless of traveler enthusiasm. Suggest alternatives (Taranaki Falls, Tama Lakes, or postpone if weather window exists later in the trip).

### Glacier Heli-Hikes — Cloud Ceiling Is Everything

**The problem:** Helicopter flights require a minimum cloud ceiling (typically 800-1,000 m above sea level at Franz Josef/Fox). The pilots make go/no-go decisions on the morning of the flight.

**Cancellation rates:** In a bad stretch of weather, heli-hikes can cancel 3-5 days in a row. In a good stretch, they run daily. The average cancellation rate is ~30-40% across the year.

**Buddi approach:** If the traveler wants a glacier heli-hike, Buddi should:
1. Schedule it for the day with the best forecast in the area
2. Have a backup day if the first attempt cancels
3. Alert the traveler the evening before if conditions look marginal
4. Have an all-weather alternative ready (rainforest walk, hot pools, Hokitika)

### Stargazing — Cloud Cover Is Binary

**The challenge:** Stargazing at Lake Tekapo / Mt John requires <30% cloud cover. There's no "moderate" stargazing — either you see the Milky Way or you don't.

**Additional factors:**
- Moon phase: Full moon washes out the Milky Way and fainter objects
- Wind: Telescope observation requires calm conditions
- Season: Winter (May-Aug) offers the longest dark hours and best Milky Way positioning
- Aurora: Kp index ≥5 for visible aurora this far north (Stewart Island is better at Kp 3-4)

**Buddi approach:** Monitor cloud cover forecasts for Tekapo. Schedule stargazing on the clearest night in the area. Inform travelers about moon phase impact. Dark Sky Experience (Mt John observatory) tours book out — reserve on the best-forecast night.

### Whale Watching (Kaikōura) — Sea State Is Binary

**The reality:** Whale Watch Kaikōura cancels trips in rough seas. Sperm whales are resident year-round, so the limiting factor is whether the boat can safely operate, not whether whales are present.

**Cancellation rate:** ~10-15% of trips cancel due to sea conditions.

**Buddi approach:** Monitor Kaikōura marine forecast. If seas are rough, suggest:
1. Try the next day (sea conditions can change quickly)
2. Do the Kaikōura Peninsula Walkway instead (coastal walking, seal colony)
3. Visit Ohau Stream seal pup colony (30 min north, weather-independent)

### Abel Tasman Kayaking — Wind Trumps Rain

**Key insight:** Light rain barely affects kayaking (you're getting wet anyway), but wind makes it dangerous and exhausting. Sea state in the bays is sheltered but open crossings (e.g., around Tonga Island) are exposed.

**Buddi approach:** Focus on wind forecast, not precipitation. Guide operators will make go/no-go calls, but Buddi can help travelers plan kayaking on calm days and walking the Coast Track on windier days.
